Spaced repetition is a strategy that involves reviewing information at increasing intervals over time. Research by Cepeda et al. (2006) found that spreading out study sessions leads to better retention than cramming. This works because every time you revisit information, your brain strengthens the memory and makes it easier to retrieve in the future.

Suggestions:

Instead of studying everything the night before an exam, review small chunks over days or weeks.
Use spaced repetition apps like Anki or Quizlet to automate review schedules.
Keep a “memory journal” where you revisit key concepts periodically.
These techniques rely on how our brains process and store information.

Active recall forces our brain to retrieve information, strengthening neural connections.
Spaced repetition prevents the “forgetting curve,” reinforcing learning just as memories start to fade.
Smith & Roediger (2013) describe spaced repetition as “a two-process system that enhances encoding and retrieval strength” (p. 830). Meanwhile, Dunlosky et al. (2013) emphasize that retrieval practice should be a core part of any learning strategy, far surpassing passive techniques like highlighting or rereading.

If you want to retain information better, waste less time studying, and truly master new concepts, start incorporating active recall and spaced repetition into your learning routine.
